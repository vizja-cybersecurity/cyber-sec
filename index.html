Essential Safety Tips

Section A Students
Section B Parents
Section C Teachers
01
Use strong, unique passwords
At least 12 characters mixing letters, numbers, and symbols. Never reuse passwords — one breach cascades to all. Consider a free password manager like Bitwarden.
Critical
02
Think before you click anything
Phishing links mimic real sites with tiny spelling differences. If a message arrives unexpectedly, hover before clicking — or go directly to the official website instead.
Critical
03
Keep personal details personal
Full name, school, address, phone, and photos should never be shared with people you only know online — even in games. Real friends don't need this to stay in touch.
Critical
04
Know how to report cyberbullying
Screenshot everything. Tell a trusted adult. Report to the platform. You never have to respond — block and step away. You are not alone, and it is not your fault.
Important
05
Audit app permissions regularly
Does a puzzle game really need your contacts and location? Go to your phone settings and revoke access that isn't genuinely necessary.
Habit
06
Understand what incognito mode does
It prevents local history from saving. It does not hide you from your school's network, your internet provider, or the websites you visit.
Habit
01
Create space for open conversations
Ask what apps and games your child uses — with genuine curiosity, not interrogation. Make it safe for them to tell you when something feels wrong online, without fear of device removal as the first response.
Critical
02
Set up parental controls thoughtfully
Tools like Google Family Link, Apple Screen Time, and Circle help manage content and schedules. Explain the rules — controls work best alongside trust, not instead of it.
Critical
03
Review privacy settings together
Go through your child's social media accounts with them. Set profiles to private, disable location sharing, and explain why these settings matter.
Important
04
Establish consistent device boundaries
No devices at meals, charge phones outside the bedroom overnight. Consistent routines protect sleep and mental health, and make rules feel less arbitrary to children.
Important
05
Secure your home network
Change the default router password. Use WPA3 encryption. Create a separate guest network for smart home devices, isolated from family devices.
Important
06
Learn alongside your child
You don't need to be an expert. Exploring new apps or games together signals curiosity rather than surveillance — and makes you the person they'll come to when something goes wrong.
Habit
01
Weave digital literacy into existing subjects
Source evaluation in English, data privacy in math, algorithmic bias in social studies. Cybersecurity doesn't need to be isolated — the best teaching happens in context.
Critical
02
Know the behavioral warning signs
Withdrawal from usual activities, visible anxiety around devices, or sudden mood changes after screen time may signal cyberbullying or online exploitation. Act early.
Critical
03
Vet tools before using student data
Verify apps comply with FERPA and COPPA before use. Never share student information in unencrypted emails or personal cloud storage. The burden of proof is on the vendor.
Critical
04
Use simulation-based learning
Platforms like Google's Interland, Common Sense Media curricula, and CISA's K-12 toolkit make concepts concrete. Students retain what they experience, not just what they read.
Important
05
Write clear classroom policies
Document expectations around device use, AI-assisted work, and communication platforms. Share them with students and parents at the start of each year.
Important
06
Partner with families
Host a parent information session early in the year. Shared expectations between school and home are far more effective than either working in isolation.

Common Online Threats

T-01High
Phishing Attacks
Fraudulent messages that impersonate trusted sources to steal credentials or financial data.
Phishing messages mimic banks, schools, or popular apps — often using urgent language and subtle domain errors (yourbank-secure.info). Never click links in unexpected messages. Go directly to the official website. Enable two-factor authentication so that even a stolen password won't be enough.
Tap to read more
T-02High
Cyberbullying
Harassment, threats, or exclusion carried out through digital platforms and devices.
Cyberbullying happens on social media, in games, via text, or in group chats. It is as psychologically damaging as in-person bullying. If it occurs: document with screenshots, block the person, and report to a trusted adult and the platform. Do not respond to the bully — that typically escalates the situation.
Tap to read more
T-03High
Malware & Ransomware
Malicious software designed to steal data, surveil users, or hold files hostage for payment.
Malware typically arrives via pirated downloads, email attachments, or misleading ads. Ransomware encrypts your files and demands payment. Defenses: keep your OS updated, use reputable antivirus software, only download apps from official stores, and back up important files regularly to an external drive.
Tap to read more
T-04High
Online Grooming
Adults who build manipulative trust with minors in games, social platforms, or messaging apps.
Warning signs: an online contact wanting to move to a private channel, requesting photos, showing unusual interest in a child's personal life, or suggesting meeting in person. Children should know they can tell a trusted adult without getting in trouble. Parents should review communication apps periodically and keep devices in shared spaces.
Tap to read more
T-05Medium
Data Breaches
Exposure of personal information when services you use are compromised by attackers.
Even reputable companies get breached. Your email, password, or payment details may be circulating without your knowledge. Check exposure at haveibeenpwned.com. Use unique passwords per site so one breach doesn't cascade into many. Enable 2FA on email and financial accounts as a first priority.
Tap to read more
T-06Medium
Misinformation & Deepfakes
AI-generated images, video, and text designed to deceive or attribute false statements to real people.
Modern AI generates convincing fakes. Before sharing shocking content, check the original source, look for image artifacts (especially hands and teeth), and use fact-checking resources like Snopes, AP Fact Check, or PolitiFact. Slow down — the impulse to share quickly is exactly what misinformation depends on.

⚠ Need help now?
If you or someone you know is being threatened or exploited online, contact the National Center for Missing & Exploited Children at 1-800-843-5678, report to the FBI's IC3 at ic3.gov, or call 911 in any emergency. School counselors are also trained for these situations — you don't have to handle it alone.
